# Salary Transparency Laws by State

## Overview

A growing number of states, cities, and localities require employers to disclose salary or pay range information in job postings, during the hiring process, or upon request. Non-compliance can result in fines and legal liability.

**Important**: This is a rapidly evolving area of law. Always verify current requirements before publishing job advertisements.

[VERIFY] All requirements listed below - laws change frequently.

## States with Pay Transparency Laws

### California

**Effective**: January 1, 2023

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Employers covered | 15+ employees |
| Disclosure required | Pay scale (salary or hourly range) |
| When | In job posting |
| Existing employees | Must provide pay scale upon request |
| Penalties | $100 - $10,000 per violation |

**Key Points**:
- Applies to any job that may be filled in California, including remote positions
- "Pay scale" means salary or hourly wage range the employer reasonably expects to pay
- Does not require disclosure of bonuses, equity, or benefits

### Colorado

**Effective**: January 1, 2021 (amended 2024)

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Employers covered | All employers with at least 1 Colorado employee |
| Disclosure required | Salary/hourly range, benefits description |
| When | In job posting |
| Remote positions | If position could be performed in Colorado |
| Penalties | $500 - $10,000 per violation |

**Key Points**:
- Broadest requirements - includes benefits description
- Remote job postings must comply if CO residents could apply
- Must also disclose to current employees when promotion opportunities arise

[VERIFY] 2024 amendments and current requirements.

### New York State

**Effective**: September 17, 2023

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Employers covered | 4+ employees |
| Disclosure required | Minimum and maximum annual salary or hourly range |
| When | In job posting |
| Job description | Required if one exists |
| Penalties | Up to $1,000 first violation, $2,000 second, $3,000 third+ |

**Key Points**:
- Applies to any position that could be performed in New York
- "Good faith" range requirement - what employer honestly believes
- Range cannot be open-ended ("$50,000 and up" not compliant)

## Remote Work Implications

### Multi-State Compliance

When advertising remote positions, consider:

1. **Where can the role be performed?**
   - If "remote anywhere in US" - comply with strictest state law
   - If limited to specific states - comply with those states' laws

2. **Conservative approach**:
   - Include salary range in all job postings
   - Include benefits description where required
   - Simplifies compliance across jurisdictions

3. **Specify location restrictions**:
   - "Remote - CA, NY, WA residents only"
   - Then only those states' laws apply

## Best Practices for Compliance

### Setting Pay Ranges

1. **Establish legitimate ranges**
   - Based on market data, internal equity, budget
   - "Good faith" requirement - what you'd actually pay

2. **Avoid overly broad ranges**
   - $50,000 - $150,000 may invite scrutiny
   - Consider separate postings for different levels

3. **Document your methodology**
   - Be able to explain how range was determined
   - Consider compensation benchmarking tools

### What to Include

**In Job Posting** (strictest compliance):
- Salary/hourly range (minimum and maximum)
- General benefits description
- Other compensation (bonus, equity) where required

**Example Format**:
```
Salary Range: $75,000 - $95,000 annually
Benefits: Medical, dental, vision insurance; 401(k) with match;
PTO; professional development stipend
Additional Compensation: Annual performance bonus eligible
```

### What to Avoid

- Open-ended ranges ("$50,000+")
- "Competitive salary" without specifics
- "DOE" (depends on experience) as only disclosure
- Ranges that don't match actual hiring range

## Compliance Checklist

Before publishing a job advertisement:

- [ ] Identify all locations where role could be performed
- [ ] Check salary transparency requirements for each jurisdiction
- [ ] Establish good-faith salary range
- [ ] Include benefits description where required
- [ ] Document compensation methodology
- [ ] Review with legal/HR if multi-state posting
- [ ] Update existing postings as laws change
